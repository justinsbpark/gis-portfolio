1. Site Location

1.1 General Description

Site 22 is East (E) Anapamau Street (St) running southwest to northeast, with this specific section being perpendicular to Laguna St on the east, and Garden St on the west.  This street is in the southeastern part of Santa Barbara County in the City of Santa Barbara, California 93101 at approximately 30 meters above sea level.1  Furthermore, the site is located in the "Eastside" region of Santa Barbara, east of State Street, classified as an Urban Climate Zone (UCZ) 5 - medium development with 1 or 2-level houses.  This report on Site 22 concentrates on the solar processes of the three residential units and features in the immediate vicinity (Figure 1) on the south side of the 300 block of E Anapamu St. 

 

1.2 Site Infrastructure

The site comprises a two-lane, asphalt road with residential units on the south side of the street and the First United Methodist Church and additional residential units on the north side of the street.  This two-way street features spaces for street parking and parking strips primarily made of compacted soil and mixed vegetation.  Concrete pavement sidewalks and pine trees line the street, creating a perfect environment for a shaded walk.  Additionally, a Santa Barbara Metropolitan Transportation District (SBMTD) bus stop is situated on the northeastern corner of the Garden and Anapamu St intersection.  Trees, hedges, and other vegetation on site create partially covered pedestrian and vehicular corridors (see Figures 2 and 5).

 

1.3 Residential Units

The three residential units that make up the site are indicated to have been constructed in the 1920s when referencing online real estate databases.2  The addresses range from (left-to-right, as seen in Figure 1) 306 Anapamu St, 308 Anapamu St, to 1134 Garden St.  The two units on the left are single-family homes while the two-story unit on the right is a multi-unit-property.  These units are constructed with a painted wood exterior with asphalt roofing; though all units are constructed with the same materials, exterior and roof colors vary by property.  The unit on the right is constructed with two entrances/walkways to the property, one on Anapamu St and one on Garden St, presumably access points for separate units within the property.  All units were observed to have some combination of a front yard, fencing, and hedges.

2. Vegetation

2.1 Vegetation Overview

While most of the vegetation on the site is confined to the three properties in Figure 1, there are several trees located on the boundaries of the area of observation, including on the north side of E Anapamu St.  Though features not included in the area of observation are typically omitted in the report, vegetation like trees play a crucial role in the solar energy cycle.  Especially when shade from out-of-boundary trees overlaps with the area of observation during certain parts of the day.  Further, vegetation is imperative to the energy budget, acting as consumers of solar energy, evapotranspiring, and providing shade to the surfaces below.

 

2.2 Hedges and Shrubs

Each of the three properties on the project site contains some form of hedges in the exterior facade of the front yard.  These hedges measure approximately 2-3 meters in height, running parallel to the sidewalk and street.  These hedges provide privacy for the residential properties, and at certain times of the day, provide shade into the yard and home, as well as into the sidewalk. 2.3 Trees

Arguably the largest providers of shade and consumers of incoming solar radiation, the three pine trees lining the site are an important asset to the site.  These pine trees, not the typical species when the word "tree" comes to mind, measure about between 8 to 11 meters in height, defined by their pine-needle leaves and expansive canopies.  Though these pine needles lack the surface area of an average leaf, they provide an adequate amount of shade for the surfaces below.  Trees in an urban environment are one of the most important factors in determining regional air temperatures, air quality, and mental well-being.

4.2.2 Conduction

In the context of the solar energy cycle, conduction at the site is defined by the movement of energy from one surface to another.  For example, surfaces such as concrete and asphalt receive solar radiation throughout the day, heating those surfaces.  As air temperature drops as the day reaches nightfall, the heat stored in these surfaces can be lost via conduction to layers underneath these surfaces such as soils, dirt, and deep underground environments.  Further, these surfaces - including buildings - can redistribute heat to cooler surfaces like other surfaces that may be covered by shade.  Urban areas lacking vegetation like trees and surfaces with high albedos, tend to have higher overall air temperatures, leading to what is known as the urban heat island effect.

 

Figure 14: Conduction in an urban environment.6

 

4.2.3 Convection

Convection at the site involves the movement of energy through thermal convection.  As the sun heats the ground, the ground redistributes this absorbed energy via convection, with the ground's warm air rising towards the atmosphere.  Simultaneously, cooler air sinks, creating a temperature gradient.  As the sun goes down, the warmer air at the site rises, replacing the air with sinking cooler air.  Additionally, based on the site's proximity to the ocean, it can experience sea and land breezes, a form of convection.  During the day the site heats up faster than the ocean, causing a land breeze in which the warmer air by the site moves towards the cooler air by the ocean.  Alternatively, the site can experience sea breeze at night, where the warmer air by the ocean moves towards the site's cooler air.  This process of convection is due to the fact that land heats up and loses heat faster than water.  Bodies of water like the ocean can retain any heat absorbed during the day more efficiently.

 

Figure 15: Convection - land and sea breezes.7

 

4.2.4 Evaporation/Evapotranspiration

In the solar energy budget of this site, evaporation and evapotranspiration serve as a consumer of solar energy through the usage of solar energy to convert water from liquid to vapor.  This process includes the energy required for plants to evapotranspire and the moisture on surfaces to evaporate.  The water vapor that is released through transpiration and evaporation then cools the surrounding environment, altering the solar energy budget and ultimately decreasing the urban heat island effect.

 

 

4. Summary

In closing, Site 22 - 300 E Anapamu Street, is a unique urban environment consisting of residential and commercial (church) properties situated on the Eastside of the City of Santa Barbara.  Classified as an urban climate zone 5 - medium development, the site features multiple 1 to 2 story, low-density residences with various forms of vegetation.  Analysis of this site highlights the importance of vegetation such as trees and hedges, with their ability to cast shade on surrounding surfaces, consume incoming solar radiation, and cool the environment through processes like evapotranspiration. 

Observed in the afternoon of a day in the winter season, it is important to note that areas such as Site 22, receive more direct solar radiation at solar noon and during summer months when the sun is positioned at a higher angle.  Additionally, while Site 22 was observed with clear skies; cloud cover and precipitation can significantly impact received and remitted solar and terrestrial radiation.  With an average air temperature of 15Â° during observation, the site felt relatively cooler than other areas, partly due to the lack of extensive vegetation at other areas that were otherwise prevalent at the site.  Finally, upon the assignment of this site, I had initially believed the church seen in Figure 2, would be the largest provider of shade but was later intrigued to learn how the sun's azimuth and elevation affected the direction of the shadows.
